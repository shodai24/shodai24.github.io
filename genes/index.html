How is genetic disease inherited?

Source: How is Genetic Disease inherited?

Dominant genetic diseases are caused by a mutation in one copy of a gene. If a parent has a dominant genetic disease, then each of their children has a 50% chance of inheriting the disease.
Recessive genetic diseases can be passed on when both parents are carriers of the same recessive disease. There is either a 25% chance that the pregnancy will inherit the trait from both parents and have the disease, a 50% chance that the pregnancy will inherit the trait from only one parent and be a carrier, or a 25% chance that the pregnancy will not inherit the trait from either parent and will not be a carrier or have the disease.
Multifactorial traits occur due to many complex interactions and combinations of maternal, environmental and genetic influences. The most common birth defects are members of this category, including congenital heart defects and cleft lip.
How does gene editing work?

Source: Perspectives on gene editing, The Harvard Gazette

Somatic gene editing targets genes in specific types of cells (e.g. blood cells). The edited gene is contained only in the specific cell type, with no other types of cells affected. Any changes are limited to the individual and are not passed down to future generations.
Germline gene editing makes changes to cells early in development, meaning all changes are passed on. The edited gene is copied to every cell including egg and sperm cells, and is passed on to future generations.
Ethical Concerns

Sources: Perspectives on gene editing, The Harvard Gazette

Nontarget Mutations
CRISPR has an increased risk for non-target effects, where genes that are not directlyy targeted are afffected by alterations. This can result in undesirable mutations that negatively affect future generations.
Enhancement and Eugenics
Should techniques like CRISPR be available for non-therapeutic use, it could allow the possibility to enhanceme specific desired traits in humans, such as appearance traits, thus leading to eugenics.
Accessibility
CRISPR is expensive and thus the potential benefits of the technology could end up localized in wealthier developed countries, leaving poorer developing countries behind and possibly giving wealthier populations genetic advantages over others.
